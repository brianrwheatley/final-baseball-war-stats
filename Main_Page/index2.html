Predicting Major League Baseball Player Performance

How does injury affect player performance?

Project Introduction

With 162 games in the regular Major League Baseball season and over 900 active players in the league, there are bound to be many injuries throughout 6 months of game play. Even the best of players will miss a few games to small injuries. Injuries ranging from small strains to UCL (Ulnar Collateral Ligament) tears and concussions that may take a player out for 5 games or for the rest of the season. However, if a player does come back to play in more games that season, how productive are they?

To answer this question, a historical dataset of WAR (Wins Against Replacement) and historical injury data for all players for the 2010 through 2021 seasons is used in a machine learning model.

The Data

Our primary sources of data were Baseball-Reference and aggregated WAR data from FanGraphs and Baseball-Reference, compiled by Neil Paine. Both sources were chosen by the completeness of data, both in terms of years and players available, as well as accuracy with limited missing data. Our data sources were fairly consistent between both sources; however, we did focus on aligning player information using player name, team, and year. Through the process, we did experience a small loss of data, around 1.5%, however our group determined that was an acceptable loss, since our data included over 18,000 data rows. Our team limited our data range to 2010, or 14 years. We felt that there was a possibility of skewed data prior to that time, due to the ongoing steroid scandals of the 1990s and early 2000s. There's also ongoing controversy with WAR numbers favoring players in earlier years due to the variance in skill levels. Injury data before the 1990s is also far from complete.
